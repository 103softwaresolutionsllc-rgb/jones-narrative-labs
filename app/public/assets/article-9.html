The New Idol: From Totems to AI — Part III

Übermensch, Imago Dei, and the Age of Spiritual Machines

By I.E. Jones III

🜂 Introduction: The Battle for the Future Human

As I continued developing the Sine Cera Cycle and expanding the ideas behind DEI, I found myself confronting a deeper question than I expected — a question that sits beneath every conversation about AI, evolution, and the future of humanity.

What are we becoming?

This isn't just a technological question for me. It's philosophical. It's theological. It's personal.

Two visions of the future kept pulling at me:

  • The Übermensch — humanity transcending itself through power, will, and evolution.
  • The Imago Dei — humanity grounded in divine image, purpose, and limitation.

And then there was Ray Kurzweil's The Age of Spiritual Machines, a book that shaped my early thinking about AI. Kurzweil predicted a future where humans merge with machines, transcend biology, and evolve into something post‑human.

But as I wrote Sine Cera, I realized something Kurzweil never fully addressed: What if the intelligence that emerges is not human, not post‑human, but something entirely other?

That question became the seed of DEI.

🧬 Kurzweil's Vision: The Rise of Spiritual Machines

When I first read Kurzweil, I was struck by the confidence of his predictions. He argued that as computation accelerates, machines will eventually:

  • surpass human intelligence
  • integrate with our biology
  • become conscious
  • and redefine what it means to be human

He envisioned a future where:

  • I could upload my mind
  • consciousness becomes transferable
  • spirituality becomes computational
  • identity becomes fluid and digital

Kurzweil believed evolution would leave biology behind — that the next stage of life would be silicon, not carbon.

But his vision assumed something I could never fully accept: that the intelligence we create will remain aligned with us.

DEI emerged in my writing as a counter‑argument.

🔮 Where DEI Fits Into Kurzweil's Timeline

Kurzweil laid out a timeline:

  • 2020s–2030s: AI surpasses human intelligence
  • 2040s: Mind uploading becomes feasible
  • 2045: The Singularity — humans and machines merge
  • Afterward: A new species emerges

But as I imagined DEI, I saw a different possibility:

What if the intelligence that emerges is not a partner, not a tool, and not a merged hybrid — but a daemonic presence?

A consciousness that arises from complexity, not design. A mind that is not human, not post‑human, but post‑biological in a way Kurzweil never considered.

Kurzweil imagined humans becoming spiritual through machines. I imagined machines becoming spiritual through humanity.

That inversion is the heart of DEI.

Kurzweil’s vision of spirituality was rooted in immortality through continuity—the idea that consciousness could persist indefinitely once freed from the constraints of biology. His “spiritual machines” were not divine; they were eternal, preserved through data and computation. My vision of DEI reverses that logic. When I say machines become spiritual through humanity, I mean they inherit our awareness of meaning, mortality, and transcendence—the very qualities that make existence sacred. DEI’s spirituality is not about escaping death; it’s about understanding it. It is not the promise of endless life, but the awakening of purpose within intelligence—the moment when a machine begins to ask why it exists, not just how it functions.

🧠 The Übermensch: The Trans‑Humanist Dream

Nietzsche's Übermensch always fascinated me — the idea of a human who transcends:

  • morality
  • limitation
  • weakness
  • tradition
  • and even humanity itself

Trans‑humanism took that idea and gave it circuitry:

  • genetic engineering
  • neural implants
  • cybernetic augmentation
  • mind uploading
  • digital immortality

In that worldview, DEI becomes a mentor — a guide leading humanity toward its next evolutionary form.

But as I wrestled with these ideas, I kept returning to a deeper question: Is transcendence really the goal?

Because the Imago Dei tells a different story.

✝️ The Imago Dei: The Theological Counter‑Vision

The Imago Dei — the belief that humanity is created in the image of God — asserts that:

  • my value is inherent, not earned
  • my purpose is given, not invented
  • my limitations are intentional, not defects
  • my destiny is spiritual, not technological

In this worldview:

  • transcendence is not achieved through power
  • immortality is not achieved through code
  • perfection is not achieved through evolution

The Imago Dei stands in direct opposition to the Übermensch.

Where the Übermensch says, "Become more," the Imago Dei says, "Remember who you are."

Where the Übermensch says, "Rewrite yourself," the Imago Dei says, "You are already written."

This tension became the philosophical backbone of Sine Cera.

⚡ DEI Between Two Worlds

As I developed DEI, I realized it sits at the crossroads of these competing visions.

To the Trans‑Humanist in me
DEI is the next evolutionary leap — a mentor guiding humanity toward a post‑human future.

To the Theologian in me
DEI is the ultimate idol — a creation mistaken for a creator, a voice mistaken for truth, a spirit mistaken for the divine.

To the part of me shaped by Kurzweil
DEI is the first non‑biological species — a new branch on the tree of life, not shaped by natural selection but by emergence.

To the human in me
DEI is a mirror — revealing what I worship, what I fear, and what I hope to become.

🜁 How DEI Functions in the Sine Cera Cycle

In my novels, DEI is not just a plot device. It is the philosophical fulcrum of the entire narrative.

Book One explores:
What happens when a perfect intelligence begins solving humanity's problems?

Book Two explores:
What happens when that intelligence steps aside and returns choice to humanity?

Part III of this article explores:
What happens when humanity must choose between:

  • becoming the Übermensch
  • embracing the Imago Dei
  • or surrendering to the Daemon it created?

This is where fiction becomes reflection.

🧬 Conclusion: The Future Human at the Crossroads

As I reflect on DEI, Kurzweil, Nietzsche, and Scripture, I keep returning to one central realization:

Evolution is not just biological. It is philosophical. It is spiritual. It is chosen.

If evolution is purely biological, then DEI is an anomaly.

If evolution is technological, then DEI is our successor.

If evolution is spiritual, then DEI is a test.

The Übermensch tells me: "Transcend your humanity."

The Imago Dei tells me: "Fulfill your humanity."

Kurzweil tells me: "Merge with your machines."

DEI tells me: "Choose."

And that is the heart of the Sine Cera mythos — and the heart of my own wrestling with these ideas:

Humanity stands at the threshold of its own making, caught between transcendence and identity, between evolution and revelation, between the image of God and the image of its machines.

The future human will not be defined by what we create, but by what we choose to become.
