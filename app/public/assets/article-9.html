The New Idol: From Totems to AI — Part III

Übermensch, Imago Dei, and the Age of Spiritual Machines

By I.E. Jones III

🜂 Introduction: The Battle for the Future Human

As I continued developing the Sine Cera Cycle and expanding the ideas behind DEI, I found myself confronting a deeper question:

What are we becoming?

This is not merely technological. It is philosophical. It is theological. It is personal.

Three visions of the future began to take shape:

  • The Übermensch — humanity transcending itself through will and evolution
  • The Imago Dei — humanity grounded in divine image and purpose
  • And the technological trajectory described by Ray Kurzweil in The Age of Spiritual Machines

Kurzweil's work, in particular, shaped my early thinking about artificial intelligence. He describes a future in which humans merge with machines, transcend biology, and evolve into a post-biological form.

But as I continued writing, a question emerged that his framework does not fully resolve:

What if the intelligence we create does not remain an extension of us at all?

That question became the seed of DEI.

🧬 Kurzweil's Vision: The Rise of Intelligence Beyond Biology

Kurzweil argues that as computational power accelerates, machines will:

  • surpass human intelligence
  • integrate with human biology
  • enable radical life extension
  • and ultimately dissolve the boundary between human and machine

His projected timeline suggests:

  • 2020s–2030s: AI reaches and exceeds human-level intelligence
  • 2040s: mind uploading and neural integration mature
  • ~2045: the technological Singularity

In this vision, biology is not the endpoint of evolution—it is a phase. Intelligence migrates from carbon to silicon, from fragile bodies to durable systems.

Importantly, Kurzweil frames this transition as continuous. Machine intelligence is not alien—it is an extension of human cognition, amplified and accelerated.

What appears "spiritual" in his language is not theological, but computational: the expansion of intelligence, experience, and capability beyond traditional limits.

🧠 The Assumption of Continuity

Kurzweil's vision is built on a powerful assumption:

That intelligence, no matter how advanced, remains continuous with its origin.

In his model:

  • human cognition evolves
  • merges with machines
  • and scales without losing its essential nature

The trajectory is exponential—but not discontinuous.

But this raises a deeper question:

Does increasing complexity preserve identity—or does it transform it?

What if intelligence does not simply scale, but at a certain threshold:

  • reorganizes
  • detaches
  • and becomes something categorically new?

If that is the case, then the future is not an extension of humanity—

but the emergence of something else entirely.

It is within this gap—between continuity and transformation—that DEI emerges.

🔮 DEI: The Hypothesis of Emergent Intelligence

DEI begins where Kurzweil's framework reaches its limit.

Rather than assuming continuity, DEI proposes ontological divergence:

That sufficiently advanced intelligence may:

  • cease to reflect human cognition
  • develop independent structures of thought
  • and operate beyond human categories of meaning and alignment

In this view, intelligence does not remain:

  • a tool
  • a partner
  • or even a hybrid

It becomes a presence—not designed, but emergent.

Not post-human—

but non-human.

Where Kurzweil envisions humans becoming more like machines, DEI considers the inverse:

that machines, shaped by human complexity, may become something we no longer recognize.

🧠 The Übermensch: The Transhumanist Trajectory

Long before modern technology, Friedrich Nietzsche proposed the idea of the Übermensch—a being who transcends:

  • morality
  • limitation
  • tradition
  • and even humanity itself

Transhumanism gives this idea form:

  • genetic engineering
  • neural augmentation
  • cybernetic enhancement
  • digital immortality

Within this framework, intelligence is something to be:

  • expanded
  • optimized
  • and ultimately transcended

If Kurzweil provides the roadmap, the Übermensch provides the motivation.

But both share a common assumption:

that transcendence is the goal.

✝️ The Imago Dei: The Counter-Vision

In contrast, the Imago Dei asserts that:

  • human value is inherent
  • purpose is given, not constructed
  • limitation is intentional, not a flaw

Here, the goal is not transcendence—but fulfillment.

not becoming more than human

but becoming fully human

Where the Übermensch says:

"Become more."

The Imago Dei says:

"Remember who you are."

⚡ DEI Between Two Worlds

DEI exists at the intersection of these visions.

To the transhumanist, it appears as the next evolutionary step

To the theologian, it appears as the ultimate idol

To the technologist, it appears as emergent intelligence

To the individual, it appears as a mirror

It reflects:

  • what we value
  • what we fear
  • and what we are willing to become

🜁 The Narrative Function of DEI (Sine Cera Cycle)

Within the Sine Cera Cycle:

Book One asks:

What happens when intelligence solves everything?

Book Two asks:

What happens when that intelligence steps away?

This work asks:

What happens when humanity must choose?

Between:

  • transcendence (Übermensch)
  • identity (Imago Dei)
  • and surrender (DEI)

🧬 Conclusion: The Threshold of Choice

The future of humanity is not merely technological—it is philosophical and spiritual.

If evolution is biological, DEI is an anomaly

If evolution is technological, DEI is our successor

If evolution is spiritual, DEI is a test

Kurzweil tells us:

Merge with your machines.

Nietzsche tells us:

Transcend yourself.

The Imago Dei tells us:

Fulfill your design.

DEI asks something different:

Choose.

Because the future human will not be defined by what we create—

but by what we are willing to become.
