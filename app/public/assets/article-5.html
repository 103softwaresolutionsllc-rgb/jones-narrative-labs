Behind the Scenes: My Writing Routine

A day in the life of a full-time author and creative.

By I.E. Jones III

🌅 "Morning: Protecting the Quiet Hours"

My day begins before the world remembers it has demands.
The early morning is sacred — a pocket of silence where my mind is still soft, unguarded, and open. I make coffee the same way every day, not because I'm a creature of habit, but because ritual steadies me. It signals to my brain: we're entering the creative space now.

Before I check messages, before I let the noise in, I sit with my notebook.
No pressure. No goals. Just a conversation with myself.

Some days it's a paragraph.
Some days it's a page.
Some days it's a single sentence that feels like a lifeline.

This is where the writing day truly begins.

✍️ "Late Morning: Drafting While the Mind Is Sharp"

Once the sun is fully up, I move to my main writing session.
This is the heavy lifting — the drafting, the scene-building, the wrestling with characters who refuse to cooperate.

I set a simple rule:
Write for two hours. No judgment. No editing. Just movement.

Some days the words flow like they've been waiting for me.
Other days I drag each sentence out like a reluctant confession.
But I show up. That's the part that matters.

I keep distractions away.
Phone in another room.
Tabs closed.
Music low and atmospheric.

This is the part of the day where I feel most like an author — not because the writing is perfect, but because I'm doing the work.

🍽️ "Midday: Stepping Away to Think"

Around noon, I step away from the desk.
Creativity needs space, and stepping away is part of the process.

I take a walk, make lunch, or read a few pages of a book that inspires me.
This is when ideas sneak up on me — in the quiet moments when I'm not trying so hard.

I jot down whatever comes:

  • A line of dialogue
  • A plot twist
  • A character revelation
  • A metaphor that feels too good to lose

These fragments often become the backbone of the afternoon session.

🧩 "Afternoon: Editing, Refining, and Rewriting"

The afternoon is for shaping what the morning gave me.

Editing requires a different version of myself — sharper, more analytical, less sentimental. I read aloud. I cut ruthlessly. I rearrange scenes like puzzle pieces until the picture becomes clear.

This is where the craft happens:

  • Tightening sentences
  • Deepening emotion
  • Clarifying motivation
  • Strengthening structure

It's slower work, but deeply satisfying.
This is where a draft becomes a story.

🎨 "Late Afternoon: Creative Cross-Training"

When my brain starts to tire, I shift to other creative tasks:

  • Brainstorming future projects
  • Sketching character notes
  • Researching settings or historical details
  • Updating outlines
  • Freewriting to loosen the mind

This keeps me connected to the joy of creation without the pressure of producing polished pages.

🌙 "Evening: Reflection and Reset"

Before I end the day, I return to my notebook — the same one I started with.

I write a few lines about:

  • What worked
  • What didn't
  • What surprised me
  • What I want to explore tomorrow

This reflection closes the loop.
It reminds me that writing isn't a sprint; it's a rhythm.
A conversation.
A lifelong practice.

Then I step away, trusting that the work will be waiting for me in the morning, ready to begin again.

⭐ Conclusion: The Routine Behind the Magic

People often imagine writing as a lightning strike — sudden, dramatic, unpredictable. But the truth is quieter. Writing is built from small, steady choices. From showing up on the days you feel inspired and the days you don't. From honoring the process even when the outcome is uncertain.

My routine isn't perfect. It evolves, shifts, adapts.
But it keeps me grounded.
It keeps me honest.
And most importantly, it keeps me writing.
